Summary

How the model gets the probabilities

FielCup does not try to guess a score. It estimates team strength, builds a scoreline distribution for each match, and turns that distribution into win, draw and loss probabilities.

The default version blends two signals: what the team has done on the pitch and the talent in the squad. That keeps the model from relying only on recent national-team results.

Four steps

The plain-English logic

  1. Results on the pitch. The model reads thousands of international matches and learns an attack rating and a defense rating for each team. Recent matches carry more weight.
  2. Dixon-Coles. This is a football-specific Poisson model. It improves low-score estimates such as 0-0, 1-0 and 1-1.
  3. Talent prior. FIFA ranking points and squad market value enter as outside signals. This helps when a national team has elite players but a noisy recent record.
  4. Simulation. For title odds, the tournament is played thousands of times in the computer. A team's title probability is how often it wins.
final strength = alpha x results + (1 - alpha) x talent
Each match

From scorelines to match odds

For a match, the model estimates expected goals for both teams. It then builds a matrix of possible scores: 0-0, 1-0, 0-1, 2-1, 1-2 and so on.

All scorelines where the home team wins are added together to get the home-win probability. Draw scorelines become the draw probability. Away-win scorelines become the away-win probability.

The same matrix also gives the likely score, xG, over 2.5 goals and BTTS.

Transparency

What the model sees and misses

It sees recent scorelines, attacking strength, defensive strength, FIFA ranking and squad value. It does not fully see injuries, age curves, fatigue, tactical changes, breaking news or tournament psychology.

The probabilities are a statistical lens, not a promise. The value is in showing the lens and being honest about its limits.

Model vs market

Why it can differ from betting markets

Betting markets mix statistics, context and money from thousands of bettors. FielCup is more explicit: it shows its assumptions. When it disagrees, the disagreement helps explain what evidence the model values.

Quick glossary

Key terms

  • Poisson: a distribution used to count rare events, like goals.
  • Dixon-Coles: a Poisson adjustment for football, especially low scores.
  • Monte Carlo: repeated simulation used to estimate probabilities.
  • Brier score: a metric for judging forecast probability quality.
  • Alpha: the blend between results and talent.
